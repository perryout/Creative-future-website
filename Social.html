What is Social Psychology?

Human beings are "social animals‚Äù (Aristotele, 350 B.C.E), and as such respond to immediate contexts.

Social Psychology is the scientific study of the behaviour of individuals in their social and cultural setting. It examines how people affect one another, how social environments, and the power of situations can influence and shape our behaviour. On how society responds with a change of behaviour in order to align with the social situation at hand, for example, what might have triggered the minds of Nazi German society, or the global philanthropic actions following a tsunami.

Social Psychologists study topics at both the intra- and interpersonal levels. The firsts pertain to the individual and include emotions, attitudes, the self-concept (who am I?), and the social self (the roles we play, social comparison, self-judgment, self in action, and culture). Interpersonal topics instead pertain to dyads and groups and include group dynamics and processes, intergroup relationships, helping behaviour, aggression, discrimination, prejudice, and attraction. (Myers, 2012; Jhangiani & Tarry, 2014; CNX Psychology, 2014)

This course cleverly presented many and different concepts, theories, and experiments developed in the field of Social Psychology. Particularly I have been fascinated by Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment (1971) which investigated the psychological effects of perceived power, and Darley, J. M., and Batson, C.D. study "From Jerusalem to Jericho" (1973) about the inconsistency between attitudes and behaviour in everyday life. (Find links below)

The first was inspired by Milgram's obedience experiment (1965) and aims to investigate further the impact of situational variables on human behaviour. The experimenter was indeed looking for explanations about the atrocities committed in the Shoah, demonstrating the powerful role that the situation can play in human behaviour.

The second instead, looked into a different variable: time. Proving that thinking about the norms does not imply that one will act on them. And this made me think about modern behaviour concerning the environment, poverty, or animal cruelty. We all know what we have to do, we know the parables, but our actions not always correspond.
